Selling Medical Devices to the NHS: The Complete Guide

Selling medical devices to the NHS means getting your product compliant (ISO 13485, UKCA/CE marking), choosing the right route to market — usually an NHS Supply Chain framework — pricing competitively against what similar devices are awarded for, and consistently finding the relevant tenders early. The NHS is a huge, structured buyer of medical devices, but it buys on quality, value and compliance, not relationships alone. Here's how to do it.

Routes to market for medical devices

There are a few ways a device gets sold into the NHS:

  • NHS Supply Chain frameworks — the main route for selling at scale across trusts
  • Direct trust tenders — individual hospitals procuring for their own needs
  • Regional collaborative hubs and clinical networks
  • Innovation routes — programmes that fast-track genuinely novel devices

For most devices, getting onto the relevant framework agreement is the priority.

Compliance: the non-negotiables

Before the NHS will buy your device, you need the fundamentals in place:

  • UKCA or CE marking appropriate to the device class
  • ISO 13485 quality management certification
  • Registration with the MHRA where required
  • Clinical evidence appropriate to the device's risk class
  • Post-market surveillance and vigilance processes

Pricing: don't guess

Medical device tenders are often heavily weighted on price. The mistake most suppliers make is pricing blind. Because award data is published, you can see what similar devices were awarded for and price within the winning range — we cover this fully in how to price a healthcare tender bid. Benchmarking against real awards is the difference between winning profitably and losing on price.

Know your competition

Public award data also tells you exactly which companies are winning device contracts in your category, in which regions, and at what value — invaluable competitive intelligence covered in how to use award data.

The hard part: finding device tenders early

Medical device tenders appear across NHS Supply Chain, Find a Tender, and individual trust portals — and if you also sell into Europe, across TED Europa too. Monitoring all of these by hand is the bottleneck most device suppliers hit.

Frequently asked questions

What certification do I need to sell devices to the NHS?

Typically UKCA or CE marking for the device class, ISO 13485 quality certification, and MHRA registration where required — plus clinical evidence appropriate to the risk class.

Is NHS Supply Chain the only way to sell devices to the NHS?

No, but it's the main route for selling at scale. Individual trusts and regional hubs also procure directly.

How competitive is NHS device procurement on price?

Very — price is usually heavily weighted, which is why benchmarking against published award values matters so much.
