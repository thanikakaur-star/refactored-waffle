Who Is Winning Healthcare Contracts? How to Use Award Data

Public contract award data tells you who won a healthcare tender, often at what value, and which buyer awarded it. Used well, it's the most powerful competitive intelligence available to a supplier — it shows you who your real competitors are, what the market pays, and where the opportunities cluster. Most suppliers ignore it. The ones who win consistently mine it. Here's how.

What is contract award data?

When a public buyer awards a tender, they usually publish an award notice — the supplier who won, the contract value, the category, and the awarding body. This is the public record of every procurement decision, and it's available across TED Europa, SAM.gov, NHS, and WHO. While tenders tell you what's available, award data tells you what actually happened.

Why award data is competitive gold

  • Know your real competitors — see exactly which companies win in your category and region
  • Understand market pricing — award values reveal what buyers actually pay, so you can price your bids competitively
  • Spot patterns — which buyers award to incumbents vs new entrants, and how often
  • Find expiring contracts — a contract awarded for 3 years will be re-tendered; knowing when is a head start
  • Validate a market — heavy award activity in a category signals real, recurring demand

Questions award data can answer

With good award data, you can answer questions that are otherwise guesswork:

  • "Who is winning the most orthopaedic implant contracts in the UK?"
  • "What's the average value of a diagnostic equipment award in Europe?"
  • "Is this buyer loyal to one supplier, or do they spread awards around?"
  • "Which competitors are expanding into new regions?"

The challenge: award data is scattered and messy

Like tenders, award notices live across separate portals in different formats, currencies, and languages. Pulling them together to answer a simple question like "who's winning in my category across the EU and US?" means manually trawling multiple systems and normalising the results by hand. It's exactly the fragmentation problem covered in manual tracking vs a platform — but for the competitive intelligence side.

How to turn award data into better bids

  • Before bidding — check who won similar contracts and at what value, then price within the winning range
  • Target accounts — identify buyers who award in your category and build relationships early
  • Track competitors — watch where rivals win and lose to find gaps you can exploit
  • Time your entry — flag contracts nearing expiry so you're ready when they re-tender

Frequently asked questions

Is contract award data really public?

For most public procurement, yes — award notices are published, typically including the winning supplier and the contract value. The work is in aggregating and normalising it across sources.

How is award data different from tender data?

Tenders are opportunities still open to bid on; awards are the record of who won past contracts. You need both — tenders for opportunities, awards for intelligence.

Can I see exactly how much a competitor bid?

Usually you see the awarded contract value rather than every bidder's price, but that award value alone is enough to calibrate your own pricing to the market.
