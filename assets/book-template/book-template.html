The Khanda is the emblem of the Sikh faith. The double-edged sword in the centre represents divine knowledge. The circle (Chakkar) symbolises God's eternal nature. The two curved swords (Kirpans) represent Miri and Piri — worldly and spiritual power.

ਖੰਡਾ (Khanda) — Double-edged sword / Sikh emblem

In 1699 at Anandpur Sahib, Guru Gobind Singh Ji asked who would give their life for their faith. Five brave Sikhs stepped forward — they became the Panj Piare (Five Beloved Ones). Guru Ji created the Khalsa, a community of saint-soldiers committed to justice and equality.

ਖਾਲਸਾ (Khalsa) — The pure / sovereign community • ਪੰਜ ਪਿਆਰੇ (Panj Piare) — Five Beloved Ones

Guru Granth Sahib Ji is the eternal Guru of the Sikhs. It contains 1,430 pages of sacred poetry and hymns composed by six Sikh Gurus and saints from Hindu, Muslim, and other traditions. Guru Gobind Singh Ji declared it the living Guru for all time.

The Five Ks

Kesh — Uncut Hair

ILLUSTRATION PLACEHOLDER

Kesh (uncut hair) is the first of the Five Ks. Sikhs keep their hair unshorn as a gift from Waheguru, respecting the body's natural form. The Dastar (turban) is worn with pride to cover and protect the Kesh — it represents sovereignty, honour, and self-respect.

ਕੇਸ (Kesh) — Uncut hair • ਦਸਤਾਰ (Dastar) — Turban

The Five Kakars

ILLUSTRATION PLACEHOLDER

Bold and easy coloring page, thick clean black outlines, minimalist kawaii line art style, cozy hygge atmosphere, rounded cute shapes, solid white background, flat vector design, no shading, no gradients, heavy line weight. The Five Ks (Panj Kakars) are articles of faith: Kesh (uncut hair), Kangha (wooden comb — discipline), Kara (steel bracelet — bond with God), Kachera (cotton undergarment — self-control), and Kirpan (ceremonial sword — duty to protect the weak).

ਕੰਘਾ (Kangha) — Wooden comb • ਕੜਾ (Kara) — Steel bracelet • ਕਿਰਪਾਨ (Kirpan) — Ceremonial sword • ਕਛੇਰਾ (Kachera) — Cotton undergarment

Vaisakhi — The Harvest Festival

ILLUSTRATION PLACEHOLDER

Bold and easy coloring page, thick clean black outlines, minimalist kawaii line art style, cozy hygge atmosphere, rounded cute shapes, solid white background, flat vector design, no shading, no gradients, heavy line weight. Vaisakhi is celebrated on April 13th or 14th each year. It marks the Panjabi harvest festival and, for Sikhs, the birth of the Khalsa in 1699. Communities celebrate with Nagar Kirtan (street processions), Bhangra dancing, and sharing festive foods.

ਵਿਸਾਖੀ (Vaisakhi) — Harvest festival / Khalsa birthday • ਭੰਗੜਾ (Bhangra) — Traditional Panjabi dance

The Gurdwara

ILLUSTRATION PLACEHOLDER

Bold and easy coloring page, thick clean black outlines, minimalist kawaii line art style, cozy hygge atmosphere, rounded cute shapes, solid white background, flat vector design, no shading, no gradients, heavy line weight. A Gurdwara ('Gateway to the Guru') is the Sikh place of worship. It has four doors to show it is open to people of every direction and background. Inside, the Guru Granth Sahib Ji is read, Kirtan (hymns) are sung, and everyone shares Langar together.

ਗੁਰਦੁਆਰਾ (Gurdwara) — Sikh place of worship • ਕੀਰਤਨ (Kirtan) — Devotional singing of hymns

Phulkari — Flower Embroidery

ILLUSTRATION PLACEHOLDER

Bold and easy coloring page, thick clean black outlines, minimalist kawaii line art style, cozy hygge atmosphere, rounded cute shapes, solid white background, flat vector design, no shading, no gradients, heavy line weight. A full-page chunky bold Phulkari embroidery pattern with big simple diamond shapes filled with cute rounded stars and chubby stylized flowers, sweet little peacock motifs with big round tail feathers, chunky wheat stalks, arranged in a simple symmetrical grid, everything big and bold and easy to color with thick heavy outlines

Phulkari means 'flower work.' It is the traditional embroidery art of Panjab, created by women using bright silk threads on handspun cotton cloth. Each region of Panjab has its own patterns. A Bagh ('garden') is a Phulkari so densely stitched that no base fabric shows through.

ਫੁਲਕਾਰੀ (Phulkari) — Flower embroidery • ਬਾਗ (Bagh) — Garden / fully embroidered cloth

Panjabi Alphabet — Gurmukhi

ILLUSTRATION PLACEHOLDER

Bold and easy coloring page, thick clean black outlines, minimalist kawaii line art style, cozy hygge atmosphere, rounded cute shapes, solid white background, flat vector design, no shading, no gradients, heavy line weight. Gurmukhi means 'from the mouth of the Guru.' This script was standardised by Guru Angad Dev Ji so that everyone could read and write, regardless of caste. The Guru Granth Sahib Ji is written in Gurmukhi. The alphabet has 35 letters and is written left to right.

ਗੁਰਮੁਖੀ (Gurmukhi) — From the Guru's mouth / Panjabi script • ਊੜਾ ਐੜਾ (Oora Aira) — First letters of Gurmukhi alphabet

Mai Bhago — The Warrior Saint

ILLUSTRATION PLACEHOLDER

Bold and easy coloring page, thick clean black outlines, minimalist kawaii line art style, cozy hygge atmosphere, rounded cute shapes, solid white background, flat vector design, no shading, no gradients, heavy line weight. Mai Bhago was a courageous Sikh warrior who led 40 soldiers back into battle at Muktsar in 1705. She fought alongside Guru Gobind Singh Ji and survived the battle. She is remembered as a symbol of bravery and a reminder that Sikh women have always been warriors and leaders.

ਮਾਈ ਭਾਗੋ (Mai Bhago) — Legendary Sikh woman warrior • ਮੁਕਤਸਰ (Muktsar) — Pool of liberation (battle site)

The Sahibzade are the four sons of Guru Gobind Singh Ji. Sahibzada Ajit Singh (18) and Jujhar Singh (14) fought bravely in battle. Sahibzada Zorawar Singh (9) and Fateh Singh (6) chose death over giving up their faith. Their courage inspires Sikhs to stand firm in their beliefs.

ਸਾਹਿਬਜ਼ਾਦੇ (Sahibzade) — Revered sons / princes of the Guru • ਸ਼ਹੀਦ (Shaheed) — Martyr
